Of Adrenaline:

One of the more interesting functions in the body, is the natural production of adrenaline. Largely responsible for a number of automatic behaviors, adrenaline is how we are able to function at a much higher level during, let's say, fight or flight. The automatic sensory behavior during fight or flight is much more than just one hormone, but adrenaline's role in increasing blood flow to muscles is one of the reasons why it is so pivotal to peak-level human performance. This is obviously handy if you get attacked, but for as long as sports have been played, it's effects can also be seen there, as being under extreme stress can cultivate extraordinary physical capabilites.

This is also my personal experience with the phenomena. From my experience with being able to deal with stress in speedrunning, I have found, that adrenaline plays a pivotal role in me being able to perform at my highest level. In-fact, I'd argue, that being able to handle stress well is caused by being able to channel the adrenaline in your body correctly. Making your focus be "avoiding disasters", causes your behavior under the immense stress to be fight, not flight. To make sure, that you are in control and that you use your hightened vigilance for negative emotions to anticipate potential disasters, and focus your attention on dealing with them.

I find, that when this is done, generally the increased blood flow in your muscles allows your muscle memory to handle the actual execution of the task much better than one could consciously. This often helps dealing with unexpected situations. In moments of surprise or mistake, there may be a moment of panic, but with good preparation, you can get yourself to react correctly, which with adrenaline, will happen very quickly, minimizing damage.

Racing other people in speedrunning isn't much better either. You are practicing for hours and hours to prepare for a match coming up, and you have that one chance, that one opportunity, to do a race against another player. Often, they're world class talent, and might even be doing the speedrunnig for a living. When you do a race, it's one go, one run-through of the game. Whoever is the fastest, wins. If it goes poorly, you're out. If you had something come up and couldn't practice, tough luck. Most of these races are live streamed to an even bigger audience than you would get yourself. Once again, it's a question of dealing with the pressure.

The way I deal with it has been a mantra: Avoid disasters. Often the margin of error is quite small, yes, and often it can feel, that any small mistake will make your goal out of reach. But that's just your mind playing tricks on you. If you focus on avoiding the big disasters, it'll be fine. People focus on small mistakes, and think about what they'll lose, but that won't ever take you down. What will take you down is those big mistakes. Obviously a minor setback is still a setback, in this case small mistakes in a level will lose me time, meaning that my final time is going to be that much slower. What people fail to consider, is that you have to hang in there and steer the ship clear of rocks, in order to take advantage of the lucky breaks and the moments of brilliance.

Often my personal best times in speedrunning come down to, not the absence of mistakes, but the absence of massive timelosses, and taking advantage of good performances elsewhere to balance out the timeloss. As one gets better and better, it's not that mistakes disappear, but what you consider a "disaster" is an increasingly smaller mistake. For example in speedrunning, starting out, a 4 minute timeloss is a big disaster, but at world record level, even 10 seconds is a catastrophy. But at that world record level, if you avoid those mistakes that you now consider big disasters, you can still net yourself a new record, even if there's timeloss in your run. Humans are never perfect, you see.
